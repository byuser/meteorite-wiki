How Meteorites Are Classified

Classification starts with a simple question: how much metal does the meteorite contain? That single trait sorts every meteorite into one of three families, which are then divided into the specific types you can browse on the Types page.

The classification tree

Group Abundance Defining trait
Stony ~94% of falls Mostly silicate minerals; includes chondrites & achondrites
Iron ~5% of falls Mostly iron–nickel metal; sorted by nickel content
Stony-Iron ~1% of falls Roughly equal metal and silicate; pallasites & mesosiderites

Chondrites vs. achondrites

The biggest split among stony meteorites is whether they melted. A chondrite never melted, so it still contains chondrules — tiny beads of once-molten rock from the solar nebula. An achondrite comes from a body that did melt, erasing the chondrules and producing rock that looks volcanic.

Falls vs. finds

Meteorites are also described as falls (seen to fall and then recovered) or finds (discovered later with no observed fall). Falls are scientifically valuable because we know they haven't spent long weathering on Earth's surface.
